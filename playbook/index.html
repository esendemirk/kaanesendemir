Playbook

How I see a successful company structure, shaped over years of watching what works and what wastes people’s lives.

Calendar & meetings

No lunch meetings. Eat. Reset. Do not turn the break into another call.

No meetings after 4:00. Not much gets done late. People need time to wind down the day.

No first-thing-in-the-morning meetings. An 8:00 kickoff is not how we start. Give people time to get oriented before the calendar owns them.

Default to 15 minutes. Get away from the 30-minute habit. Aim for 15. Cap normal meetings at 30 minutes.

Two-hour meetings are not OK for regular work. Status meetings, syncs, standups, demos-as-status: none of that should run two hours. No exceptions for the usual meeting types.

IM before a meeting. If a quick message can handle it, use IM. A lot of calendar clutter is BS that should have been a short chat.

Email before you book time. Prefer email (or a written thread) before a meeting is set. Meetings are for what writing cannot settle cleanly, not the default first move.

Vacation

No tiered vacation cliffs. The usual model (two weeks until year five, then a third week at year ten) is out. Tenure should open time steadily, not in rare jumps.

Start with two weeks (10 days). Then every completed year of work earns you one extra vacation day for the years ahead. Think of it like restricted stock units, but for time off: each year of tenure unlocks another day into your annual bank.

Examples:

  • Year 0 (start): 10 days
  • After 1 year: 11 days
  • After 2 years: 12 days
  • After 19 years: 29 days
  • After 20 years: 30 days

Stay longer, earn more rest. The bump lands each year you complete, not in five-year cliffs.

Holidays

Generous holiday schedule. People take the public holidays of the country they work from. Local calendars matter; we do not pretend one HQ calendar fits everywhere.

Coverage still matters. Teams plan so critical work is covered. Holidays are real days off, not silent on-call guilt.

In the U.S., align with kids being out. We take off pretty much any day kids are typically off school, plus the usual bank holidays, so parents can actually be with their children instead of juggling PTO theater around every school break.

Remote-first

Remote-first by default. The cheapest office is no office. We do not lease a permanent HQ unless we are genuinely huge and can afford one without pretending it is required for culture.

No strict hybrid quotas. If the work can be done remotely, we do not invent fake structure like “two days a week in office” or “three days a week or else.” That is theater.

Work from elsewhere is allowed. Want to work out of the country for a month? Fine, if the role can stay remote and the work still gets done. Trust outcomes, not badges on a door.

Shared meetup days, not attendance theater. People still need to meet people. We plan occasional shared days (monthly, or every couple of weeks) for team building, demos, and face time. Rent a WeWork conference room or similar for those days. Not a daily commute mandate. Strongly encouraged so the team stays human, not a punishment calendar.

See the company, see the world

International is a feature. If we grow into an international company with locations, people should be empowered to visit them, not stuck forever in one seat on a map.

Explore operations and cultures. Want to spend time at the Turkey location, or Mexico, or wherever we plant a flag? You should be able to go, work from that office, meet coworkers from different places, and learn how the company actually runs elsewhere.

That is career development and team building in one move: see the business, see the world, bring the perspective home.

Leadership access

Directors, VPs, and higher-ups stay accessible. Title is not a moat. People should be able to talk to senior leaders without a gatekeeper maze or the feeling that leadership lives in a different company.

Build real familiarity across levels. Meetup days, lunches, and other shared events are not only for peers. They exist so everybody gets to know each other, including leadership. No executive silo. No “you only speak to your manager’s manager once a year at all-hands.”

Open calendars for connection, not theater. Skip-level conversations and casual time with higher-ups should be normal and welcome. If someone wants a meeting with a director or VP, the default is yes when the ask is good-faith, not “protect the calendar at all costs.”

Onboarding & swag

Onboarding is not an afterthought. Day one should feel intentional. A real kit: laptop bag with the logo, quality basics people will actually use, not a sad sticker pack.

Yes, it is a little cliché. People still care. They post it. That is marketing and belonging at the same time. We lean into company swag on purpose.

Intern program

Interns year-round, not summer-only. A constant influx of interns, even when the cost is high. Learning pipelines beat once-a-year theater.

Every intern gets a mentor. Not a floating buddy on Slack. A real person they assist while they learn, with clear ownership and feedback.

Food & fuel

Company / team lunches. People should not have to pay out of pocket or brown-bag every shared meal. When we gather, food is covered.

Coffee, tea, and light snacks are free. Especially early on, while we are still private and growing. Small fuel, big signal that people are looked after.

Appreciation

Shout people out on purpose. Wins should not die in a private DM. Keep people motivated with public recognition.

Internal newsletter of appreciation. A regular note that makes people feel seen: who shipped, who helped, who went above and beyond.

Benefits

Health insurance, fully company-paid (high-deductible plan). Default coverage so nobody is uninsured. Even a small employee premium like $20 should not come out of pocket. The company pays the premium. It is still a high-deductible plan, but it is paid in full so everyone has a baseline.

401(k) with a generous match. Help people build wealth while they build the company.

Wellness stipend. No company gym (remote-first). Instead, a monthly credit toward a gym or similar wellness membership. Not unlimited, but real enough to use.

Pay

Pay is transparent. Every role and level has one number for the year, not a secret range. Spectrum bands (“$X to $Y depending”) are theater. This year’s senior engineer pay is what every senior engineer makes. Same for VP, manager, and every other level: one published rate, one bonus structure for that seat.

Same level, same pay. Everybody at a given level is paid exactly the same. No side deals that make peers unequal for the same job.

Talking about salary is fine. No stigma. No “we prefer you keep compensation private.” Equality only works if people can see it.

Job matrices get published. Interns through executives can look up the chart: what an intern makes at each grade, what an application developer makes, what a product owner makes, what a manager of software engineering makes, what a manager of product management makes, and the rest. You should know the ladder before you climb it.

Bonuses

Your promised bonus is the floor, not a hope. Total compensation includes a base bonus percentage by management seniority tier. That number is what you can count on. Company performance does not claw it back or water it down with “missed the metric” theater.

Tiers by seniority. Different management levels carry different base bonus percentages. The package you accepted is the minimum you get.

Upside when the company does well. Strong company years can mean more than the base. Bad or messy company metrics do not turn your promised bonus into a smaller surprise.

Stock

Employees get company stock. There is a real stock program so people own a piece of what they are building, not only a paycheck.

Performance

No formal performance reviews. Throw the annual rating theater out. Forced stacks, “solid contributor,” “meets expectations,” “exceeds” ladders: they do not mean much, especially for people already performing at a high level who still get marked as just doing the job.

If it is bad, you will hear it. Your manager calls out real problems when they show up. No waiting for a calendar ritual.

If it is fine, keep going. There is no useful split between “just doing your job” and “going above and beyond” on a form. Do the work. Get paid fairly. Move on.

Learning & events

Certifications are paid when justified. If the person’s manager and that manager’s manager agree the cert is justified for the role, the company pays.

Industry events count as work. Summits, tech conferences, hackathons, and similar. Ask your manager. If it is justifiable and they agree, it is an excused work day (work outside the office), not a personal day burn.

Travel for approved events is covered. Airfare, hotel, getting there and staying there: paid when the manager green-lights the event. We encourage people to go learn in public, not sit home because expense forms feel scary.
