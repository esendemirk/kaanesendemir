How I’d run it

The point of this board — a living note on leadership, not a policy PDF.

This page is a vision board, not a manifesto carved in stone. It’s how I think about leading people if I’m responsible for a team, a product org, or a company of my own.

I care about shipping serious work — enterprise systems, careful AI adoption, education that sticks. I also care that the humans doing that work don’t burn out to prove a point.

What follows is a running set of principles. I’ll add to it. I’ll revise it when experience teaches me better.

Non-negotiables

The lines that don’t move when pressure shows up.

People are not the backlog. Urgency is real. Cruelty isn’t a delivery strategy.

Judgment stays human. Especially with AI. Tools accelerate; people decide what ships, what’s safe, and what’s worth doing.

Say the hard thing early. Ambiguity eats more time than a clear “no” or a clear “not yet.”

We don’t perform busyness. Busy calendars are not a proxy for impact. Outcomes are.

Time and meetings

Boundaries that protect deep work and real life.

No lunch meetings as a default. Eat. Walk. Reset. If something is truly urgent, we name why — we don’t colonize the one break people have.

No meetings after hours unless production is on fire. Real incidents get a response. Habitual evening standups do not.

Meetings earn their slot. Agenda or it doesn’t happen. Decision or it becomes an email. “Quick sync” is not a strategy.

Deep work is protected. Focus blocks aren’t optional decoration on a calendar — they’re how architecture and teaching quality stay high.

Vacation and rest

Generous time off, without the guilt trip.

I want a generous vacation policy — and a culture that doesn’t punish people for using it.

Rest is not a reward for surviving crunch. It’s part of how we stay sharp enough to do enterprise-grade work and teach the next person well.

When someone’s out, we cover them without making them feel they owe the company their phone on the beach. If the system only works when everyone is always online, the system is wrong.

Team life

Events, belonging, and an environment people don’t dread.

I’d rather invest in a few meaningful team moments than a constant stream of forced fun.

Celebrate real wins. Make space for people to know each other as humans. Keep rituals light enough that introverts aren’t exhausted and extroverts aren’t bored.

An enjoyable work environment isn’t ping-pong tables. It’s psychological safety, clear ownership, and the feeling that careful people can speak up without being labeled difficult.

Still writing

This board stays open on purpose.

If you’re reading this, you’re seeing a draft of how I want to lead — as an architect, as an educator, and someday as someone accountable for a larger org.

I’ll keep adding chapters: hiring bars, AI use norms, on-call ethics, how we treat students and juniors, what “done” means when the stakes are production.

If something here resonates — or you’d push back — find me on LinkedIn. This is meant to be lived, not laminated.
